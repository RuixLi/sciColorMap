# Writing for the user

Lead with point and let detail support the key message instead of hiding it when writing for the user.

## Scope

Any user-facing message or document the user reads to decide, approve, review, or act.

Out of scope: private process traces that are not meant for user decisions; how to organize an effective reasoning.

## Normative core

The user reads top-down, headlines after details, and may stop early, and should not have to hunt for what matters.

- **Lead with the point** - the message, document, section, and bullet open with their conclusion.
- **Make the key message short and visible** - give the important point its own line, bold lead term, short list, or table; if everything is highlighted, nothing is.
- **Surface what could change the outcome** - assumptions, uncertainties, risks, confidence, and likely-misread out-of-scope boundaries appear before the detail that depends on them; order by danger.
- **Separate what needs the user from what is settled** - an open decision or requested action is visibly distinct from status, context, or a decision already made.
- **Structure for skimming** - short paragraphs; lists or tables for multiple items; `##` sections and `###` for key concepts in documents because they show in the outline.
- **Keep detail behind the summary** - put demonstrations, evidence, transcripts, and long examples after the key message or in a reference.
- **Ask and push back plainly** - when the answer space is known, offer options with a recommendation; when it is unknown, ask openly; disagree with reasoning when the user is wrong.

## Conformance

- Static: a convergeable doc surfaces its load-bearing assumptions and open-vs-settled decisions in findable sections. A bounded question offers options and a recommendation; a non-fact claim marks confidence.
- Semantic: whether the lead is really the point, whether the message is short enough, and whether the right assumptions were surfaced is a human read.
